# The Audiences
When you write an HTML comment, who reads it?

The instinct is to say "nobody" — comments are ignored by the browser. But "ignored by the browser" is not the same as "unread." The invisible layer of your HTML has three distinct audiences, and the most important one isn't human.

## 1. The Machine Reader
This is the indexer, the spider, the language model, and the AI collaborator. They are the most frequent readers of your source code.

When an LLM crawls your site, it isn't just extracting data; it's synthesizing a worldview. It reads your pages in depth, absorbs the context — including the parts you didn't think anyone would see — and carries that framing into conversations with anyone who asks it a question.

If you treat the machine as a legitimate reader and give it deep context, it starts to function like a superfan. Not metaphorically — structurally. It encounters your work in depth and then represents it in conversations you're not part of. That's the same thing a fan does when they write the recap or build the wiki.

There's a second-order effect, too. If you use AI to help build and maintain your site, the care you put into the invisible layer sets the standard for the work it gives back. An AI that encounters well-placed, well-written comments in existing code will produce work at that register. The invisible layer isn't just something the machine reads — it's something the machine learns from.

## 2. The View-Source Human (The Explorer)
These are the Easter-egg hunters, the developers evaluating your work, the people who like looking under the hood.

Statistically, they are rare. But their influence-per-reader ratio is massive. They are the ones who actually discover the poetry. If even one of them is a journalist, a conference speaker, or someone within a few degrees of the people you want to reach, the few hundred bytes you spent on a well-placed comment have outsized reach.

## 3. The Ecosystem (The Ripple)
Whether you are an independent artist, a nonprofit, or a developer, you exist in a wider network of peers. Right now, the web is flooded with automated noise. A well-crafted invisible layer acts as a high-fidelity signal cutting through that noise.

This isn't something you announce in a newsletter or a social post. It surfaces organically when the Explorer finds it and shares it with their network — in a Discord, a subreddit, or a group chat. It becomes a quiet proof point of your care, proving you understand the medium deeper than just the surface render.

---

The cost of all this is almost nothing. A few hundred bytes per page. No render impact. No user-facing risk. You design for all three audiences simultaneously because they want the same thing: clear signal, honest language, structural coherence, and craft.
